# CAPM & Asset Pricing

The Capital Asset Pricing Model and the theory of risk and return.

CAPM's one-line claim: **you only get paid for the risk you can't diversify away.**

$$
E[R_i] = R_f + \beta_i\,(E[R_m] - R_f)
$$

- $R_f$ — the risk-free rate (what you earn for taking no risk)
- $\beta_i$ — how much the asset moves when the market moves
- $E[R_m] - R_f$ — the market risk premium

## Key terms — drill them

  { front: 'Systematic risk', back: 'Market-wide risk (recessions, rate shocks) that diversification cannot remove. CAPM says this is the ONLY risk that earns a premium.' },
  { front: 'Idiosyncratic risk', back: 'Company-specific risk (a CEO scandal, a failed product). Diversification washes it out — so the market pays you nothing for carrying it.' },
  { front: 'Security Market Line (SML)', back: 'The line E[R] = R_f + β(E[R_m] − R_f). Assets above it are cheap (positive alpha), assets below it are expensive (negative alpha).' },
  { front: 'Alpha (α)', back: 'Return above what CAPM predicts for the asset’s beta. Persistent positive alpha is the holy grail of active management — and extremely hard to find.' },
  { front: 'Market risk premium', back: 'E[R_m] − R_f: the extra return investors demand to hold the risky market portfolio instead of the risk-free asset. Historically ~4–6%/yr for equities.' },
  { front: 'Risk-free rate (R_f)', back: 'The return on an asset with zero default risk — in practice, short-dated government bills. The anchor every risky return is measured against.' },
]} />

## Worked example

A stock has β = 1.4. With a risk-free rate of 3% and an expected market return of 8%:

$$
E[R] = 3\% + 1.4 \times (8\% - 3\%) = 10\%
$$

If your research says the stock will actually return 13%, CAPM says it offers **+3% alpha** — either
you've found a bargain, or your forecast (or CAPM itself) is wrong. Quant careers are built on
telling those two apart.
