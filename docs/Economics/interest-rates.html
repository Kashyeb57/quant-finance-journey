Interest Rates

How rates are set, the yield curve, and their effect on markets.

Einstein (allegedly) called compound interest the eighth wonder of the world. Whether he said it or not, the math earns the title — a small rate difference, given enough time, becomes an enormous outcome difference:

FV=P(1+rm)mtFV = P\left(1 + \frac{r}{m}\right)^{mt}FV=P(1+mr​)mt

The gap between the two curves is money your money made. Notice it barely exists for the first few years, then dominates — that is why quants say time in the market beats timing the market.

Experiments to run:

  1. The time lever. Set 8% for 10 years, note the interest earned — now drag to 40 years. Quadrupling the time multiplies the interest by far more than 4×. Compounding is nonlinear.
  2. The rate lever. At 30 years, compare 4% vs 8%. Doubling the rate roughly quadruples the final interest. This is why the level of interest rates moves entire markets.
  3. Compounding frequency. Switch annual → daily. The gain is real but modest — frequency matters much less than rate and time. (The limit is continuous compounding, FV = Pe^rt.)
  4. Zero deposit test. Set the monthly deposit to $0 — pure compounding on the initial amount. That curve is discounting in reverse: it is exactly how bond prices relate to yields.

Why quants care​

  • Discounting: every future cash flow is worth less today by exactly this math run backwards — present value = FV / (1 + r)ᵗ. All of fixed income sits on this.
  • The yield curve plots the rate r for each maturity t. Its slope (steep, flat, inverted) is one of the most watched recession signals in macro.
  • Policy rates (Fed, ECB, NRB) anchor the short end of the curve; expectations and term premia shape the rest.
