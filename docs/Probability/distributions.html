Random Variables & Distributions

Discrete and continuous distributions and their properties.

The normal (Gaussian) distribution is the workhorse of quantitative finance — returns models, option pricing, and confidence intervals all lean on it. Its density is

The shaded area is the probability. Try a=−1, b=1 with μ=0, σ=1 — you should see ≈68.3%, the first band of the 68–95–99.7 rule.

Experiments worth running:

  1. Verify the 68–95–99.7 rule. With μ=0, σ=1 set a=−1, b=1 → ≈68.3%. Then a=−2, b=2 → ≈95.4%.
  2. Slide μ around — the whole curve shifts but never changes shape. The mean is pure location.
  3. Crank σ up — the curve flattens and spreads. Probability mass leaks into the tails, which is exactly what "more risk" means for a return distribution.
  4. Tail risk: set a=−8, b=−3 with μ=0, σ=1. That tiny sliver is a "3-sigma event" — rarer than 0.2%. Markets produce them far more often than the Gaussian says they should (fat tails!).
