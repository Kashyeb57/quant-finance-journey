# Stochastic Calculus

Brownian motion, Ito's Lemma, SDEs, and martingales for modeling market randomness.

The single most important SDE in finance is **geometric Brownian motion** — the model behind
Black–Scholes:

$$
dS_t = \mu S_t\,dt + \sigma S_t\,dW_t
$$

Its solution (via Itô's Lemma) is

$$
S_t = S_0 \exp\!\left[\left(\mu - \tfrac{\sigma^2}{2}\right)t + \sigma W_t\right]
$$

Things to notice while you play:

1. **The expected path is a lie you never live.** The dashed red curve is E[Sₜ] = S₀e^μᵗ, yet almost
   no individual path tracks it. Averages hide the violence of randomness.
2. **The −σ²/2 correction is real.** Set μ = 0% and σ = 60%: more than half the paths finish *below*
   S₀ even though the drift is zero. That's the volatility drag term (μ − σ²/2) at work.
3. **Volatility scales with √t.** The fan of paths widens like a square root, not a straight line —
   double the horizon and uncertainty only grows by √2.
4. **Paths are jagged everywhere.** Brownian motion is continuous but nowhere differentiable —
   there's no slope to measure, which is precisely why ordinary calculus fails and Itô calculus exists.
